URBAN MORPHOLOGY DASHBOARD — 3-Minute Presentation Script
==========================================================

[~0:00 – 0:30] INTRODUCTION
----------------------------
Hi everyone. Today I'm presenting the Urban Morphology Dashboard — an interactive visual analytics tool built to explore New York City's building landscape.

The data comes from NYC's PLUTO dataset, which contains detailed records on over 815,000 buildings across all five boroughs — Manhattan, Brooklyn, Queens, the Bronx, and Staten Island. Each record includes attributes like year built, number of floors, building area, lot area, land use type, and zoning district.

Because visualizing 815K points interactively would be too heavy for the browser, I used a Python preprocessing script to create a representative stratified sample of 5,000 buildings — 1,000 per borough — for the interactive charts, while full-dataset aggregate statistics are precomputed and displayed in the summary cards at the top.


[~0:30 – 1:15] THE VIEWS
--------------------------
The dashboard has six coordinated views.

Starting at the top, the Summary Cards show full-dataset statistics — total buildings, average floors, average building area, lot area, year range, and zoning types. When filters are applied, these update to reflect the filtered subset with the overall numbers shown as a baseline for comparison.

Row one has two large views. On the left is a Scatterplot showing Year Built versus Number of Floors. Each dot represents a building — its size encodes building area, and its color encodes borough. On the right is a Geographic Distribution dot map that plots every building by latitude and longitude, colored by floor count using a diverging color scale. This gives a spatial overview of where tall versus short buildings are concentrated.

Row two has three charts. The Trend Line shows median floors per decade, broken down by borough, revealing how building heights have evolved over time. The Land Use bar chart is a horizontal bar chart showing average number of floors sorted by land use category — from residential to commercial to industrial. And the Zoning chart is a grouped bar chart showing the top 10 zoning districts with bars broken down by borough, so you can compare zoning patterns across the city.


[~1:15 – 2:15] INTERACTIONS & CROSS-FILTERING
-----------------------------------------------
The key feature of this dashboard is full cross-filtering — selecting data in any one view updates all other views.

Let me demonstrate. I can brush a year range on the scatterplot — say, 1990 to 2020 — and immediately the dot map, trend line, land use chart, zoning chart, and summary cards all update to show only buildings from that era.

I can click a land use category — for example, "Multi-Family Elevator" — and every other view filters down to just those buildings. The scatterplot now shows only elevator buildings, the map highlights where they're clustered, and the trend line shows their height trends over the decades.

Similarly, clicking a zoning district in the grouped bar chart filters all views to that zone.

For borough comparison, instead of a simple dropdown, I've implemented multi-select borough chips. I can click Manhattan and Brooklyn to compare just those two boroughs side by side. The trend line highlights their lines while dimming others, the zoning chart shows only their distributions, and the map zooms into their geographies. I can toggle any combination of boroughs on or off.

All active filters appear as removable tags in the filter bar, and I can clear individual filters or clear all at once.


[~2:15 – 2:50] DESIGN DECISIONS
---------------------------------
A few design decisions worth noting. I used a diverging five-color palette — warm tones for Manhattan and the Bronx, cool tones for Brooklyn and Queens, and a neutral gray for Staten Island — to ensure the boroughs are visually distinct and accessible.

The charts use viewBox-based responsive SVGs, so they scale fluidly to any screen size without clipping. And each interactive chart — the land use bar, zoning bar, and scatterplot — excludes its own filter when computing its data, so that clicking a bar highlights the selection while keeping all other categories visible for comparison.


[~2:50 – 3:00] WRAP-UP
------------------------
To summarize, this dashboard enables multi-dimensional exploration of NYC's 815,000 buildings through six linked views with brushing and cross-filtering, multi-borough comparison, and full-dataset context — all in a single, scroll-free page. Thank you.
